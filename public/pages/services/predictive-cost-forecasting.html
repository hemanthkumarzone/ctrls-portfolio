The problem

Month-end surprises are
preventable — if you look ahead

The most expensive moment in AI FinOps is month-end, when the bill arrives and the number is wrong. Over budget. Unexplained. Unfixable. By then, every decision that created the overrun was made 2–4 weeks ago — and no amount of incident review changes what you already spent.

Predictive Cost Forecasting moves the conversation from post-mortem to pre-intervention. By modelling your AI spend trajectory 30, 60, and 90 days ahead — using real telemetry from your workloads, usage trends, growth signals, and planned infrastructure changes — it surfaces budget breach risks when there's still time to act.

The goal is not a perfect prediction. The goal is early enough warning that your team can choose between course-correction options — throttle a workload, defer a training run, enable a circuit breaker, shift to spot instances — rather than simply receiving a bill that's already been spent.

A forecast that is 5% off but gives you 14 days to act is infinitely more valuable than a perfectly accurate bill that arrives on the 1st of the month when nothing can be changed.

📬
Month-end bill shock

Finance teams receive invoices they cannot reconcile. Engineering teams receive cost overruns they cannot explain. The root cause is almost always a decision made weeks earlier — invisible without forecasting.

📊
Linear budget planning

AI budgets are set at the start of the year based on last year's actuals — but AI usage grows non-linearly. Within two quarters, most AI budgets are already mis-calibrated against actual trajectory.

🎲
No scenario visibility

Product teams make launch decisions without knowing what a 3× usage spike means for the AI cost line. Engineering teams approve training jobs without knowing how they affect the quarterly forecast.

⏰
Too late to act

Most teams get a forecast in the last week of the month — when 80% of the budget is already spent and the only remaining lever is emergency action that disrupts production.

Annual planning — the strategic horizon

The annual forecast is a strategic planning tool — used in budgeting cycles to project full-year AI infrastructure spend under different growth and technology assumptions. It combines historical trend, seasonal patterns, growth rate scenarios, and known technology changes (model price reductions, infrastructure migrations) into a range of plausible annual outcomes.

Rather than a single number, the annual forecast produces a planning range — the optimistic, base, and pessimistic scenarios — giving finance and leadership teams a defensible basis for annual AI budget setting.

Budget guardrails

Forecast-driven alerts that
fire before you breach budget

Forecast accuracy only has value if the right people are alerted at the right time with enough context to act. Budget guardrails translate forecast signals into team-level action triggers.

🚨

Multi-threshold alerts

Configurable alert thresholds at 75%, 85%, 90%, and 100% of monthly budget — each triggering different escalation paths. Early threshold alerts go to team leads; 90%+ alerts escalate to engineering management and FinOps.

🎯

Forecast-triggered (not spend-triggered)

Alerts fire when the forecast crosses a threshold — not when actual spend does. If the forecast shows you'll hit 100% by month-end on day 14, the alert fires on day 14 — giving 16 days to act, not 0.

📋

Alert context package

Every alert includes: current forecast vs budget, the top 3 cost drivers responsible for the trajectory, and the top 3 recommended actions ranked by impact on the forecast. Alerts are actionable, not just informational.

🔀

Team-scoped guardrails

Budget guardrails are set per team, per cost category, and per product — not just at the aggregate level. A team approaching their allocated budget triggers alerts routed specifically to that team lead, not a global alert that no one owns.

⚙️

Automated response options

Optional automation triggers when forecast thresholds are crossed — activate a circuit breaker, apply pending right-sizing recommendations, or throttle a specific service — reducing time to action from hours to seconds.

Platform integration

Forecasting closes the loop
on every platform pillar.

Predictive forecasting is the strategic layer that gives every platform action a consequence — connecting today's monitoring signals to tomorrow's budget outcomes.

PillarHow forecasting integratesStrategic outcome
📡 Monitoring Monitoring telemetry is the primary input to the forecasting engine — every cost signal, usage metric, and anomaly event updates the forecast model in real time. When monitoring detects a new spend pattern (a new service, a deployment change, an API key rotation), the forecast immediately incorporates it and recalibrates the month-end projection. The forecast is always based on the most current picture of your infrastructure — not a snapshot from last week's batch job. Monitoring changes are reflected in the forecast within 15 minutes.
⚙️ Optimization Every optimisation recommendation is quantified in forecast terms before you approve it — "applying this right-sizing recommendation will reduce the month-end forecast from $148K to $141K." The optimisation engine surfaces recommendations ranked by their projected forecast impact, not just their dollar savings, so teams act on the changes that matter most to the budget trajectory. Optimisation decisions are made with full forecast context. Teams know exactly how much each pending recommendation improves their budget position — making approval decisions both faster and more confident.
🔀 Orchestration The orchestration engine uses the forecast as a dynamic budget signal — when the forecast shows a team approaching their budget ceiling, the orchestration engine proactively shifts batch workloads to spot instances, reduces scheduled job frequency, and defers non-urgent training runs before the threshold is reached. Orchestration becomes forecast-responsive rather than just rule-based. The orchestration engine prevents budget breaches autonomously — without waiting for a human alert response. Teams find their forecasts automatically stabilise when approaching budget limits, driven by intelligent workload scheduling.
🗑️ Waste elimination Waste elimination actions are prioritised by their forecast impact — the waste items eliminated first are those that most reduce the month-end forecast, not just those with the highest monthly cost. A waste item that peaks on the last 10 days of the month has a higher forecast impact than one that is uniformly distributed, and the engine prioritises accordingly. Waste elimination becomes forecast-aware — teams eliminate waste in the order that most improves their budget trajectory, not in arbitrary cost order. The effectiveness of the waste programme is measured in forecast improvement, not just total savings.

Common questions

Forecasts are available from day one of connecting your data sources — but their accuracy improves over time as the models learn your specific workload patterns. In the first 7 days, we use cross-workload pattern matching to bootstrap estimates while your models warm up. By day 14, workload-specific trend models are active. By day 30, seasonal decomposition is incorporated. By day 90, full ensemble forecasting with seasonality, event conditioning, and growth modeling is running at peak accuracy. We display the forecast confidence interval prominently so you always know the uncertainty range — wider in the first weeks, narrowing as models mature.
You can add planned events to a calendar in the platform — specifying the date, the expected impact type (traffic spike, training job, infrastructure migration), and a magnitude estimate (e.g. "+30% traffic for 7 days"). The causal impact model incorporates these events into the forward forecast, and the platform also uses historical data from similar past events to calibrate the magnitude estimate. If you don't know the exact magnitude, you can run the scenario with low, medium, and high impact assumptions to see the range. Events can also be added retroactively to improve the model's understanding of what drove past cost changes.
Forecast accuracy is measured using MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) — the average percentage difference between the forecast made N days ahead and the actual outcome. Across our customer base: 7-day forecast MAPE: ±1.8%; 14-day MAPE: ±3.2%; 30-day MAPE: ±5.4%; 90-day MAPE: ±9.8%. These are medians — individual accuracy varies based on workload stability. Stable, predictable workloads achieve significantly better accuracy; highly variable or rapidly growing workloads see wider confidence intervals. Your account dashboard shows your personal forecast accuracy history so you can track model improvement over time.
