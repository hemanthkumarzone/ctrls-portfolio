The problem

Over-provisioning is the default.
Right-sizing is the discipline.

When engineers provision AI workloads, they do what any rational person does under uncertainty: they add a safety margin. A model that ran at 4 vCPU in testing gets 8 in production, just in case. A training job that peaked at 2 GPUs gets 4, because nobody wants to explain a slow run to a deadline.

Individually, these decisions are reasonable. Collectively, they compound into systematic over-provisioning across your entire infrastructure — and the over-provisioned capacity sits idle, billing every second, while the workloads themselves run at 30–40% of capacity.

Right-size Recommendations analyses actual resource consumption across a 30-day window and produces precise, confidence-scored recommendations for every workload — with projected savings, safety analysis, and one-click or automated application. No engineering judgment required. No performance risk accepted.

Right-sizing is the single highest-ROI optimisation action available for most AI teams. No architectural changes, no code changes — just matching provisioned resources to actual needs.

How it works

From raw telemetry to
actionable recommendation
in five steps

Every recommendation is backed by 30 days of actual usage data, statistical analysis, safety validation, and a confidence score your team can trust.

1

Collect usage telemetry

CPU, memory, GPU, storage, and network metrics are collected at 1-minute granularity from the Kubernetes metrics API, cAdvisor, and DCGM for GPU workloads. No agent changes or workload modifications required.

2

Build usage distribution model

Usage percentile profiles are constructed per workload per resource type — P50, P90, P99, P99.9 — across the 30-day window. Seasonal patterns and bursty workloads are identified and factored into the profile.

3

Calculate safe right-size target

The recommendation engine calculates the optimal resource target: usage ceiling (P99 for CPU, P99.9 for memory) plus a workload-type-specific safety buffer. Buffer defaults are tuned per resource type and can be overridden per workload.

4

Score confidence and safety

Each recommendation receives a confidence score based on data recency, usage variance, workload criticality, and the gap between current and recommended values. High-variance or critical workloads receive conservative buffers and lower confidence scores that trigger human review.

5

Present with projected impact

Recommendations are surfaced with: current resource configuration, recommended configuration, projected monthly saving, confidence score, estimated SLA impact (always zero for correctly scored recommendations), and one-click or automated application.

Bursty workloads are identified by their usage variance score — a high-variance workload has a wider gap between P50 and P99 usage. For these workloads, the right-sizing engine applies a larger safety buffer (configurable, default 40% above P99 for high-variance workloads vs 20% for stable ones) and assigns a lower confidence score. The recommendation still results in savings — just more conservative ones — and the lower confidence score flags it for human review before application. Workloads with unpredictable spikes beyond P99.9 are flagged as "review manually" and excluded from automated application.

You can also define "frozen" workloads that are excluded from right-sizing recommendations entirely, such as stateful services with manual scaling requirements.
