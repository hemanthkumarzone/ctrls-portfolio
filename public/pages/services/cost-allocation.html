The problem

Shared AI infrastructure is
impossible to split without a system

A Kubernetes cluster running 12 teams' AI workloads generates a single bill. A shared GPU node pool serves inference, training, and embedding jobs simultaneously. A single API key funds calls from three product features. Without a structured allocation model, every dollar of this shared cost is invisible — it lands in someone's "cloud bill" with no connection to the team or product that generated it.

The consequence isn't just an accounting problem. Teams that don't see their own cost can't be held accountable for it, can't budget accurately for next quarter, and have no incentive to optimise. Finance can't reconcile AI spend to business outcomes. FinOps can't identify which team's workloads are driving growth in the bill.

Cost Allocation is the foundation that makes every other FinOps capability meaningful. Right-sizing only matters if you know which team owns the workload. Waste elimination only works if you know who to notify. Reporting only serves finance if every dollar is attributed to a line on a chargeback statement.

Cost allocation is not a reporting exercise — it is a governance discipline. When teams see their real cost, their behaviour changes. That is the ROI.

Allocation methods

Four methods. One for
every allocation scenario.

Different resource types need different allocation approaches. Our engine supports all four standard methods — configurable per resource type, per namespace, and per cost category.

Direct Attribution

The gold standard of allocation — when a resource can be unambiguously traced to a single team or product, its cost is attributed directly with no sharing or splitting required. Direct attribution produces the most accurate, most defensible allocation numbers.

Our platform maximises direct attribution coverage by combining tag-based matching, namespace ownership mapping, API key tracing, and Smart Tagging inference — so that the maximum possible fraction of your cost is directly attributed before any shared cost methodology is applied.

Used for: exclusively-owned pods, team-specific API keys, dedicated node pools, and workloads with clear namespace ownership
Produces the cleanest chargeback statements — every attributed item has a one-to-one owner with full lineage
Combined with Smart Tagging, direct attribution typically covers 65–80% of total AI spend
Remaining shared cost (typically 20–35%) is then allocated using one of the three shared cost methods

Proportional Allocation

Distribute shared cost in proportion to each tenant's actual resource consumption — the fairest method for heterogeneous workloads where different teams use different amounts of the shared resource.

Proportional allocation uses real usage data (CPU consumed, memory used, GPU hours utilised, request volume) — not request sizes or reserved capacity — so teams that actually use more pay more, and teams that use less pay less.

Used for: shared Kubernetes clusters, shared GPU node pools, shared vector databases, shared API gateways
Most accurate reflection of actual usage — penalises over-provisioning naturally and rewards efficient workloads
Configurable per resource dimension: CPU-proportional, memory-proportional, GPU-proportional, or combined weight
Usage window is configurable — daily, weekly, or monthly average prevents single-day spikes from distorting allocation
Allocation formula
# Proportional: by actual usage share team_share = team_usage / total_usage team_cost = shared_pool_cost × team_share # Example: shared GPU pool $12,400/mo team_A_gpu_share = 0.52 # 52% of GPU used team_A_cost = 12400 × 0.52 = $6,448

Even Split Allocation

Divide shared cost equally across all active tenants — the simplest method, most appropriate for shared services where the benefit is roughly equal regardless of consumption volume.

Even split works best for cluster-level overhead (monitoring stacks, ingress controllers, CoreDNS) where the cost of the service is fixed and all teams benefit equally from its existence. It avoids penalising heavy users of compute for the existence of shared infrastructure.

Used for: cluster overhead, monitoring infrastructure, logging stacks, ingress controllers, shared tooling subscriptions
Prevents perverse incentives where heavy compute users are penalised for shared infra costs they don't control
"Active tenant" definition is configurable — by namespace activity, by request volume threshold, or by explicit team membership list
Often combined with proportional for compute costs and even split for overhead — giving a fair blended allocation
Allocation formula
# Even split: equal share per active tenant team_cost = shared_pool_cost / active_tenants # Example: monitoring stack $3,840/mo # 6 active teams each_team = 3840 / 6 = $640 per team

Custom Weighted Allocation

Assign explicit percentage weights to each tenant for specific shared cost pools — ideal when business agreements, SLAs, or strategic decisions define the allocation split rather than actual usage.

Custom weights are defined once per shared pool and applied automatically every billing period. They are versioned — changes take effect from the date specified and are retroactively applied to historical data if requested. Finance teams can propose weights; engineering teams can review and approve.

Used for: contractually-agreed cost splits, strategic investment allocation, regulated chargeback models, shared platform cost agreements
Weights are versioned and audit-trailed — every change is logged with who made it and when it took effect
Can be combined with proportional as a floor/ceiling: proportional up to a cap, then weighted for the remainder
Approval workflow available: Finance proposes weights, Engineering approves, platform applies — all in-platform with no email chains

Shared cost engine

The hardest part of allocation:
shared infrastructure

Shared costs — monitoring stacks, ingress controllers, shared GPU pools, logging infrastructure — are the most disputed and least accurate part of any allocation model. Our shared cost engine handles them systematically with four configurable policies.

Policy 01 · Proportional

Resource-proportional split

Distribute shared cost in proportion to each tenant's actual resource consumption from the shared pool — the most accurate method for heterogeneous workloads.

shared × (team_usage / total_usage)
Policy 02 · Equal

Equal distribution

Divide shared cost equally across all active teams — appropriate for fixed-cost shared services where usage levels don't determine the cost of the service.

shared / active_team_count
Policy 03 · Weighted

Custom weight allocation

Assign explicit percentage weights per team for specific shared pools — for contractually-agreed or strategically-defined allocation splits.

shared × team_weight_pct
Policy 04 · Platform overhead

Direct overhead exclusion

Exclude shared costs from all team allocations and report them as a separate platform infrastructure line — common for internal platform teams.

reported as: platform_overhead

Each shared cost pool can use a different policy. GPU node overhead uses proportional, monitoring uses even split, and tooling subscriptions use custom weights — all configured independently, all running automatically.

Platform integration

Allocation is the foundation
every pillar stands on

Every platform capability — monitoring, optimization, orchestration, waste elimination — produces better outcomes when cost allocation is accurate. Allocation is not a reporting feature; it is infrastructure.

PillarHow accurate allocation powers this capabilityOutcome
📡 Monitoring Monitoring dashboards are filtered by team allocation — each team lead sees only their allocated cost, not total cluster spend. Anomaly detection uses allocated cost baselines, so a spike in "team-inference" spend triggers an alert to the inference team, not a generic platform alert. Without allocation, anomalies route to no one. Anomalies, budget alerts, and cost trend notifications always reach the team that owns the spend. No alert falls into an unowned inbox.
⚙️ Optimization Right-sizing recommendations are ranked by savings impact within the owning team's allocated spend — so team leads see their own optimisation opportunities in priority order. Savings realised from applied recommendations are tracked against the team's allocated baseline, giving a verifiable efficiency score per team. Teams own their optimisation backlog. Savings are attributed to the team that acted — giving a clear, team-level ROI for the FinOps programme.
🔀 Orchestration The orchestration engine applies team-level and business-unit-level budget guardrails to its routing decisions — a team approaching its allocated budget threshold triggers more aggressive spot routing and scheduling deferral to avoid crossing the cap before month-end. Budget guardrails only work if allocation is accurate. Orchestration operates within team budget constraints automatically — no overspend surprises at month-end, no manual intervention required when a team approaches their cap.
🗑️ Waste elimination Every identified waste item is attributed to an owning team via the allocation model. The waste elimination engine routes approval requests to the correct team lead and tracks savings by team. Without allocation, waste items sit in an unowned queue with no approval path and no one to notify. Waste approval workflows complete faster because they go to the right person. Monthly waste savings are reported by team — enabling team-level accountability for waste reduction.
